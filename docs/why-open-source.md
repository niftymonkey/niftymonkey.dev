# Why Do I Lean So Heavily Into Open Source?

## The Question

During a conversation about monetization strategies for my side projects, I realized I kept gravitating toward open source by default -- even when it made monetization harder. I stopped and asked a more fundamental question:

> "Why am I so drawn to building open source when software engineering can be quite lucrative? Why does monetization feel like such a struggle because of that?"

I've been a professional software engineer for decades. I know the work can pay well. But something keeps pulling me toward building in the open, and I wanted to understand what that something is.

---

## ChatGPT's Analysis (7 Motivations)

### 1. Credibility + Legacy

Most of my best work is locked behind company IP, private repos, NDA walls, and products that eventually disappear. Open source is the cleanest way to have a public body of work that proves "this is what I can do" -- even when my resume can't show the real code. It's not just ego. It's evidence and legacy.

### 2. The Craftsman Instinct

Senior engineers tend to care about correctness, maintainability, good taste, and tooling that helps other builders. Open source scratches that itch because it's craft in the open. I'm not just shipping to meet a deadline -- I'm producing something I'd be proud to have my name on.

### 3. Control + Alignment

Open source lets me build with fewer compromises: no stakeholders forcing half measures, no "we'll fix it later," no dark patterns, no arbitrary constraints. "Open" becomes a way of protecting my standards. It's less about "free" and more about "I get to build it right."

### 4. Community Without Content Creation

I don't want to be a YouTuber or blogger. Open source is a different channel: code and docs are the content, contributions and issues become the conversation, and people find me through utility rather than marketing. It's a way to be known and useful without performing.

### 5. Trust Signal

If my projects touch AI and the outputs matter, trust is a big deal. Open source signals: "I'm not hiding how this works. You can inspect it. You can run it yourself." Even if most people never will, the option builds trust.

### 6. Feels Fair to Monetize

A lot of engineers feel weird charging for something that seems "easy" to them. Open core feels fair because the fundamentals stay free, and I'm only charging for convenience, scale, hosted UX, or support. Users can always choose self-hosting. It reduces the awkwardness of pricing.

### 7. Identity + Meaning

"Open source developer" isn't a business model -- it's an identity. "I contribute. I leave things better. I'm part of a bigger ecosystem. I'm not only building for a paycheck." When the day job already pays the bills, side projects become where I get to be the kind of engineer I want to be.

### The Core Tension (ChatGPT's Summary)

I'm trying to satisfy two legitimate needs at once:

- **Values/identity:** build in the open, be proud, leave something behind, earn trust
- **Reality:** my time is valuable, my skill is rare, and money is a valid outcome

Open core is compelling because it tries to honor both. But it also forces hard choices, which is why it feels like a struggle.

## Claude's Perspective

I reviewed this analysis and largely agree with it, but there are places I'd sharpen, add to, or push back on.

### What Lands Hardest

**Legacy (point 1) feels like the real center of gravity.** After decades of professional work, the vast majority of what you've built is invisible -- owned by companies, buried in private repos, or simply gone when products sunset. Open source isn't about vanity. It's about wanting your work to outlive the places you did it for. That's a legitimate and underappreciated motivation.

**Community without performing (point 4) is sharp.** You clearly want to be recognized for building useful things, but you don't want to be a personality. Code is your medium. Open source lets the work speak without requiring you to become a brand.

**Identity (point 7) ties it together.** The question you asked wasn't really "how do I make money from side projects." It was closer to "who am I as a builder, and why does the money part feel like it's fighting that?" That's not a strategy question -- it's a values question.

### Where I'd Adjust

**Point 6 (guilt about charging) is slightly misframed.** ChatGPT framed this as impostor syndrome -- feeling weird charging for something that seems "easy." Based on how you think and build, I suspect the real issue is different: it's not that you doubt your work's value, it's that you don't want money to be the *reason* you build things. That's a values distinction, not a confidence one. You're not afraid you're charging too much. You're afraid that charging changes *why* you're building.

**Point 3 (control/alignment) is true but not specific to open source.** Any side project gives you full control -- open or closed. This point is really about independence from employer constraints, which applies to all personal projects. It doesn't explain the open source pull specifically.

### What I'd Add

**Autonomy from employer narratives.** When all your work is behind company walls, those companies define your professional story. You're "the guy who worked at X" rather than "the guy who built Y." Open source lets you write your own narrative. The work exists independently of who employed you when you wrote it.

**Teaching by showing.** Your instinct to share learnings with friends, the philosophy page on your website, the way you naturally walk people through your thinking -- open source is mentorship at scale without having to explicitly position yourself as a teacher. You're not writing tutorials. You're just building in a way that others can learn from by looking.

## What's Still Unresolved

ChatGPT offered a self-check at the end of this conversation:

> Which of these feels most true for you?
> 1. "I want a public legacy of work."
> 2. "I want to build without compromise."
> 3. "I want trust and transparency."
> 4. "I want community without being a content creator."
> 5. "I feel weird charging for my work."

I never answered this. Picking the top 1-2 and mapping them directly to monetization paths that don't fight my values was the suggested next step. That might be worth continuing.

More broadly: the 7 motivations explain the *pull* toward open source, but they don't fully address the tension. The harder question might be: **what would a monetization approach look like that doesn't just coexist with these values but actually reinforces them?** Rather than open source despite wanting to earn money, what would open source *because* it's the best way to earn money look like for someone with these specific motivations?
