# The Notebook: statement of intent

## What it is

A permanent, personal collection of engineering thinking: a lab notebook for software, not a blog. It is where I think in public about the craft: what I tried, what the evidence showed, and what I now believe as a result.

Prose is the most common medium, not the definition. An entry may be an essay, a long cited dossier, an interactive explainer, or a video with real written substance around it.

## Why it isn't a blog

A blog is a stream. Posts are dated, published once, and quietly rot. Nobody goes back, and nobody's supposed to. The notebook inverts that:

- **Entries are kept, not shipped.** Each one is something worth keeping, not a post to feed a schedule. There is no cadence to satisfy.
- **Entries change.** When my thinking moves, the entry is re-reviewed. The date carries a re-review mark, not a "last updated" apology. Revision is the point, not an embarrassment.
- **Nothing is deleted.** When an entry is genuinely superseded, it stays: dimmed and struck, linked to what replaced it. Being wrong on the record is part of the method; erasing it would be dishonest. Supersession reads as integrity, never as a warning.
- **Claims carry their evidence.** Assertions expose their sources, context, and caveats. Confidence is stated, not implied, so a reader can see how strongly I hold a thing and why. The first entry's rated evidence cards are one expression of this, not a template every entry must follow.

## What it's for

- To reason honestly in the open, and to let the reasoning improve over time.
- To value getting *closer to true* over getting there *first*.
- To leave a trail others can inspect: the sources, the counter-evidence, the questions still open.

## The feel

Quietly crafted, not decorated. The metaphor is an engineering logbook rendered as a terminal: monospace chrome, system type, dark-native with light mode as a first-class equal, and one sage accent used only to mark what is *live*: the current section, a re-review, a running example. Delight lives in the precision and the voice, not in effects. It should feel unmistakably mine, and unmistakably made by hand.

## The test of a good entry

Not "is this polished enough to publish?" but:

- Would I want to *reread* this in a year?
- Does it show its evidence, and admit what's weak?
- If I turn out to be wrong, will the record show that honestly?

If yes, it belongs in the notebook.
